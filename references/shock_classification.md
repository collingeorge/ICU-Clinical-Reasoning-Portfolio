# Shock Classification & Hemodynamic Decision Framework  
**CTICU Attending-Level Reference**  
Version 1.1 — December 2025

---

# Overview

Shock is defined as **circulatory failure resulting in inadequate tissue oxygen utilization**, regardless of blood pressure.  
Classification is essential because **each shock type has distinct physiology, diagnostic signatures, and therapeutic imperatives**.

In the CTICU, most patients exhibit **mixed shock physiology**, requiring integration of invasive monitoring, serial echocardiography, and metabolic markers.

---

# 1. Core Diagnostic Approach (Attending-Level Algorithm)

## Step 1 — Confirm Tissue Hypoperfusion
Shock exists if any of the following are present:
- MAP < 65 mmHg with end-organ injury  
- Rising lactate (> 2 mmol/L)  
- Low ScvO₂ (< 65%) or SvO₂ (< 60%)  
- Low urine output (< 0.5 mL/kg/hr)  
- Cold, mottled extremities, altered mentation  

**MAP alone is insufficient.**  
A patient can have shock physiology with MAP 70–75 if DO₂ is inadequate.

---

## Step 2 — Determine the Primary Physiologic Derangement
Use four anchors:

### 1. Preload (Filling Pressures)
- CVP, RAP, PCWP  
- RV/LV diastolic collapse on echo  
- Septal position  
- IVC diameter and variation  

### 2. Pump Function (Cardiac Output)
- LVOT VTI (every shock evaluation must include this)  
- EF (supportive but not primary)  
- RV function (TAPSE, RV S', septal shift, PAPI)  
- Lactate and ScvO₂ trends  

### 3. Afterload (Systemic Vascular Resistance)
- Warm vs cold extremities  
- Diastolic BP and pulse pressure  
- Calculated SVR (if invasive CO available)  

### 4. Obstruction
- Tamponade, tension pneumothorax, massive PE  
- Echo: RV dilation, septal D-sign, pericardial effusion with RA/RV collapse  

---

## Step 3 — Map Physiology to Shock Category
All shock fits one of four archetypes (with mixed states common):

1. **Hypovolemic** — low preload  
2. **Cardiogenic** — low pump function  
3. **Obstructive** — external barrier to flow  
4. **Distributive** — low SVR ± variable cardiac output  

---

# 2. Shock Types (CTICU Attending-Level Detail)

---

# I. Hypovolemic Shock  
**Mechanism:** Reduced intravascular volume → decreased preload → decreased stroke volume → low CO.

### Common Causes
- Hemorrhage (postoperative bleeding, GI bleed, retroperitoneal)  
- Dehydration, third-spacing  
- Maldistribution secondary to inflammation  

### Hemodynamic Profile
| Parameter | Finding |
|----------|---------|
| Preload | Low (CVP < 8, small IVC, LV underfilled) |
| CO | Low |
| SVR | High (compensatory vasoconstriction) |
| Skin | Cool, clammy |
| Lactate | Elevated |

### Echo Hallmarks
- Small LV with vigorous contraction (“kissing ventricles”)  
- Collapsing IVC  
- Low VTI (< 15 cm) that rises with fluid challenge  

### Management Priorities
1. **Immediate volume resuscitation** (balanced crystalloid; blood if hemorrhagic)  
2. **Stop the source** (surgery, IR embolization, GI consult)  
3. Avoid vasopressors unless MAP < 65 after volume optimization  
4. Use dynamic fluid responsiveness (PLR, VTI) rather than static pressures  

---

# II. Cardiogenic Shock  
**Mechanism:** Pump failure → decreased CO despite normal or elevated preload.

### CTICU-Specific Causes
- Ischemic cardiomyopathy  
- Post-cardiotomy LV or RV failure  
- Takotsubo cardiomyopathy  
- Acute valvular failure  
- Myocarditis  
- Arrhythmia-mediated cardiogenic shock  

### Hemodynamic Profile
| Parameter | Finding |
|----------|---------|
| Preload | Normal–High |
| CO | Low |
| SVR | High |
| Skin | Cool |
| Lactate | Elevated |

### Echo Hallmarks
- Reduced LV or RV systolic performance  
- Low VTI (< 15)  
- RV dilation with septal shift in RV failure  
- Declining TAPSE, RV S', or FAC  

### Metabolic Markers
- ScvO₂ < 60–65%  
- Rising lactate despite MAP correction  
- Low Pulsatility Index (ECMO) or PAPI (RV failure)  

### Management Priorities
1. **Inotropes:** Dobutamine (first-line), milrinone (if RV failure or pulmonary hypertension)  
2. **Afterload reduction:** Nitroprusside, nitrates, ACE inhibitors (as tolerated)  
3. **Pressor support:** Norepinephrine to maintain MAP ≥ 65  
4. **Mechanical Circulatory Support (MCS):**  
   - IABP (afterload reduction for LV failure)  
   - Impella for LV unloading  
   - VA-ECMO for profound biventricular failure  
5. Treat underlying cause (PCI, rate control, antiarrhythmics)  

---

# III. Obstructive Shock  
**Mechanism:** Extracardiac blockage prevents adequate preload or adequate forward flow.

### CTICU-Specific Causes
- Cardiac tamponade (post-op pericardial effusion, post-pacing wire removal)  
- Massive PE  
- Tension pneumothorax  
- Severe dynamic LVOT obstruction  

### Hemodynamic Profile
| Parameter | Finding |
|----------|---------|
| Preload | High or falsely elevated |
| CO | Low |
| SVR | High |
| Skin | Cool |

### Echo Hallmarks
**Tamponade:**  
- RA/RV diastolic collapse  
- Dilated IVC without variation  
- Swinging heart (acute blood accumulation)

**PE / RV Outflow Obstruction:**  
- RV dilation  
- Septal D-sign  
- McConnell’s sign  
- RV pressure overload (TR gradient > 35–40 mmHg)

**Tension Pneumothorax:**  
- Hyperinflated lung  
- RV compression from intrathoracic pressure  

### Management Priorities
1. **Relieve the obstruction** — the only definitive treatment  
   - Pericardiocentesis (tamponade)  
   - Thrombolysis or embolectomy (massive PE)  
   - Needle decompression/chest tube (tension pneumothorax)  
2. Temporizing measures for PE:  
   - Norepinephrine + dobutamine  
   - Inhaled pulmonary vasodilators  
3. Avoid excessive PEEP; worsens RV afterload  

---

# IV. Distributive Shock (Most Common in ICU)
**Mechanism:** Pathologic vasodilation → reduced SVR → maldistribution of blood flow.  
Often accompanied by **high-output cardiac physiology** early, then myocardial depression later.

### Primary Etiologies
- Sepsis (dominant cause in CTICU)  
- Anaphylaxis  
- Neurogenic shock  
- Adrenal crisis  
- Post-cardiopulmonary bypass vasoplegia  

### Hemodynamic Profile
| Parameter | Finding |
|----------|---------|
| Preload | Normal–Low |
| CO | High early; low late |
| SVR | Profoundly low |
| Skin | Warm flushed early → cool late |

### Echo Hallmarks
- Hyperdynamic ventricle early (EF > 70%, VTI high)  
- Normal or low filling pressures  
- Vasoplegic physiology often coexists with post-bypass myocardial stunning  

### Management Priorities
1. **Early norepinephrine** (first-line pressor)  
2. **Add vasopressin** to reduce catecholamine dose and restore vascular tone  
3. **Hydrocortisone** for refractory shock or suspected adrenal insufficiency  
4. **Fluid resuscitation:** guided by dynamic measures, stop early if not responsive  
5. Broad-spectrum antibiotics within **60 minutes** for suspected sepsis  

---

# 3. Mixed Shock States (Common in CTICU)

Mixed physiologies are the rule, not the exception.

### 1. Septic + Cardiogenic Shock
- Sepsis-induced myocardial depression  
- Low SVR with low or normal EF  
- Requires **pressors + inotropes** simultaneously  

### 2. RV Failure + Distributive Shock
Seen in:
- PE  
- Pulmonary hypertension  
- High PEEP ventilation  
- RV infarction  

Treatments differ:
- Reduce PEEP  
- Inotropes, inhaled vasodilators  
- Norepinephrine for MAP support  

### 3. Post-Cardiotomy Shock
Combination of:
- LV dysfunction  
- RV dysfunction  
- Vasoplegia  
- Hypovolemia  
- Pericardial tamponade risk  

Requires structured escalation:
- Optimize preload → inotropes → pressors → inhaled agents → MCS  

### 4. Hemorrhagic + Distributive  
Trauma, GI bleed + septic component.

# 4. Summary Table (Attending-Fidelity)

| Shock Type | Preload | CO | SVR | Echo Signature | First-Line Therapy |
|------------|---------|----|-----|----------------|--------------------|
| Hypovolemic | Low | Low | High | Small LV, kissing walls | Fluids/Blood |
| Cardiogenic | Normal/High | Low | High | Low VTI, LV/RV dysfunction | Inotrope + Pressor ± MCS |
| Obstructive | High or pseudo-high | Low | High | RV dilation, tamponade signs | Remove obstruction |
| Distributive | Normal/Low | High → Low | Low | Hyperdynamic LV | Norepinephrine + Vasopressin |

---

# Key Takeaways for Rounds

1. **Always start with DO₂/VO₂ physiology — not MAP.**  
2. **Echo is mandatory** for accurate shock classification (VTI > EF).  
3. **Pressors treat distributive shock; inotropes treat cardiogenic shock.**  
4. **Fluids help hypovolemia but harm RV failure or tamponade.**  
5. **Mixed shock is common; treat each component in priority order.**
